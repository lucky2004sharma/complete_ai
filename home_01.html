WRITE FOR THE READER, NOT FOR YOURSELF

What to include on each page

Say clearly what you do, who it's for and how it helps — lead with the most useful information first, the same way a news article puts the key facts in the opening lines.

HOMEPAGE

  • A headline that says what you offer in a handful of words
  • One or two lines on how it helps the visitor
  • A clear next step ("Try the tool", "Get in touch")
  • A short testimonial or trust signal if you have one

SERVICES / TOOL PAGE

  • What it does and the specific problem it solves
  • Any pricing or limits, stated upfront
  • The steps involved, in plain order

ABOUT PAGE

  • Why the project exists and who built it
  • Anything that supports credibility
  • A human touch — a name, a photo, a short note

CONTACT PAGE

  • An email address or contact form
  • Response hours, if relevant
  • Anything else visitors ask for most often

HOW TO WRITE FOR THE WEB

Five habits that make pages easier to read

Keep it scannable

Short paragraphs and bullet points beat long blocks of text.

Use clear headings

Break sections up so a skimming reader still gets the gist.

Speak to "you"

Write toward the reader's problem, not just your own story.

Skip the jargon

Plain words move faster than technical ones ever will.

Lead with verbs

"Resize", "download", "compare" — action words tell readers what happens next.
